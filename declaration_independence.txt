
    <h2>Background</h2>
    <p>The Declaration of Independence was drafted as the American colonies sought freedom from British rule. Tensions had been escalating due to oppressive taxes and lack of representation in British Parliament, culminating in the desire for self-governance and independence.</p>

    <h2>Key Events</h2>
    <p>
        <strong>Drafting</strong>: The Continental Congress appointed a committee to draft the declaration, with Thomas Jefferson as the principal author.<br>
        <strong>Approval</strong>: On July 4, 1776, the Continental Congress formally adopted the Declaration of Independence, marking the birth of a new nation.
    </p>

    <h2>Content</h2>
    <p>The Declaration of Independence consists of several key parts:
        <ul>
            <li><strong>Preamble</strong>: An introduction stating the necessity of independence.</li>
            <li><strong>Philosophical Foundation</strong>: Asserts the rights to life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness and the idea of government deriving power from the consent of the governed.</li>
            <li><strong>Grievances</strong>: Lists the colonists' complaints against King George III, detailing the injustices that led to the decision to seek independence.</li>
            <li><strong>Conclusion</strong>: Officially declares the colonies free and independent states, absolved from all allegiance to the British Crown.</li>
        </ul>
    </p>

    <h2>Outcome</h2>
    <p>The adoption of the Declaration of Independence signaled the start of the American Revolution, which ultimately led to the formation of the United States of America. The document laid the foundation for American democracy and has had a lasting influence on the nation's values and principles.</p>

    <h2>Significance</h2>
    <p>The Declaration of Independence is a seminal document in American history, symbolizing the country's commitment to freedom and equality. It has inspired countless movements for independence and human rights around the world.</p>
